Every card in Beast Card Clash belongs to an element. Elements tie the card system to the game's Colombian biodiversity theme and create strategic variety in how you build your deck and play your turns.

## The elements

There are five playable elements, plus a special `NONE` type for untyped cards:

```gdscript game_constants.gd
enum Elements {
    NONE,
    AIR,
    EARTH,
    ENERGY,
    FIRE,
    WATER
}
```

## Thematic background

Beast Card Clash is built around the Universidad Nacional de Colombia (UNAL) and the richness of Colombian native wildlife. Each element maps to a dimension of this theme:

| Element | Thematic connection |
|---|---|
| **Air** | High-altitude biodiversity, the Andes, the Condor's domain |
| **Earth** | Terrestrial ecosystems, tropical forests, ground-dwelling fauna |
| **Energy** | The life force of megadiverse environments, biodiversity richness |
| **Fire** | Volcanic highlands, tropical heat, transformation |
| **Water** | Colombia's river networks, wetlands, aquatic life |

The four playable [characters](/mechanics/characters) — Bear, Condor, Chameleon, and Frog — each have their own ecological niche that aligns naturally with one or more elements, though the game does not restrict which element cards a character can hold.

## Elements in battle

Elemental type is one of the two properties on every card (alongside `value`). During the **Referee phase**, the Referee state compares the cards all players have played and resolves damage and deaths based on that comparison.

<Note>
The exact elemental interaction logic — whether one element beats another or modifies damage — is handled entirely inside the Referee state of the battle state machine. For a full walkthrough of how the Referee resolves a round, see the [Battle](/mechanics/battle#referee) page.
</Note>

What you can count on as a player:

- A higher `value` on a same-element card always plays a role in the outcome.
- Mixing elements in your deck gives you flexibility but may reduce the chance of capitalizing on favorable matchups.
- Building a focused single-element deck creates consistency at the cost of predictability — experienced opponents may read your strategy.

## Building around elements

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Single-element decks">
    Committing to one element lets you build a coherent strategy around that element's thematic strengths. A Fire deck, for instance, pushes high values and aggressive plays. A Water deck favors adaptability. The tradeoff is that a focused deck is easier for opponents to anticipate.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Mixed-element decks">
    Blending two or three elements increases unpredictability and lets you respond to different situations. You trade some consistency for versatility. This is often a good approach when playing against multiple bots with unknown deck compositions.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="NONE cards">
    Cards with element `NONE` are wildcards. They do not align with any elemental matchup, which means they neither gain nor lose from elemental interactions. Consider them neutral tools — their raw `value` is what matters most.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

<Tip>
Pay attention to which elements the bots tend to favor in a given session. Bot decks are randomly generated at the start of each match, but you can read patterns from the cards they play in early rounds and adjust your own card selection accordingly.
</Tip>
